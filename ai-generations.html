The Core Argument

AI is a force multiplier, not a force generator. It amplifies what you already understand. The danger is not that AI is too powerful — it is that the systems meant to build human expertise before AI arrives have been quietly eliminated.

01 — The New Reality

AI Is the Operating System
of Global Business

Every major industry has crossed a threshold. AI is no longer a tool professionals choose to use — it is the infrastructure through which work gets done. Proposals, analysis, logistics, legal review, code, financial modelling — the baseline expectation has shifted permanently.

The working generation — Millennials and Gen Z professionals already in the field — did not adopt AI from a position of comfort. They were forced into it to sustain relevance. And what they discovered changed everything: AI could accomplish in hours what had previously taken weeks. The adoption was not enthusiastic. It was survival.

02 — The Fundamental Gap

Process Knowledge Is
Not Transferable by Curriculum

In the petroleum industry — in any technical industry — the value of experience is not the title or the degree. It is the accumulated instinct that tells you when a number is wrong, when a contract clause is suspicious, when a workflow is about to break. That instinct is built by participation. By being inside the process.

The working generation integrated AI into their workflows precisely because they already understood those workflows. They knew what to automate, what to question, and what the AI could not see. The fresh college graduate has studied theories of processes — not the processes themselves.

This is not a failure of the graduate. It is a structural mismatch between the pace of AI adoption and the pace of curriculum reform.

The Real Danger: Universities are producing AI-literate graduates who lack the domain literacy to deploy that AI meaningfully. Employers are discovering this gap in real time — and they have no roadmap to close it.

03 — The Structural Crisis

AI Eliminated the
Apprenticeship Layer

The analyst. The field assistant. The junior associate. These roles were not just job titles — they were the institutions through which tacit knowledge was transferred across generations. Supervised participation. Mentored error. The slow accumulation of judgment through proximity to experienced professionals.

Those are precisely the roles AI is eliminating or compressing first. Why hire three junior analysts when one senior analyst with AI can produce the same output in half the time?

The consequence is both logical and catastrophic: if AI removes the apprenticeship layer, the next generation has no sanctioned pathway into the domain expertise that makes AI useful in the first place. You cannot skip from graduation to senior-level AI-augmented work. The middle layer was the ladder. The ladder is being taken away.

04 — The Coming Generation

Gen Alpha: AI-Native,
Domain-Blind

Generation Alpha will arrive in the workforce already fluent in AI interfaces, prompting, and tool orchestration. They will not need to be taught how to use AI. That is the one thing we will not need to worry about.

The concern is different: tool fluency is not domain knowledge. A student who has used a calculator their entire life still needs to understand what they are calculating — and why. Gen Alpha may interface with processes they have never participated in, using AI to produce outputs they cannot validate, in domains they have never been trained to question.

This is not a criticism of a generation. It is an indictment of the institutional response — or lack of it — from educators, policymakers, and industry leaders who are watching this gap form and have not yet agreed on how to close it.

The Paradox: Every generation has learned processes from scratch. But this is the first generation where the entry-level roles that built that foundation are being structurally eliminated faster than new ones are being designed. The pipeline is not slow — it is broken.

05 — The Path Forward

Survival Requires
Deliberate Process Exposure

The graduates who will survive this transition are not those who studied AI theoretically, nor simply those who are "AI-literate." They are those who deliberately embed themselves in real processes — through internships, apprenticeships, freelance engagements, or entrepreneurial exposure — early and aggressively enough to build domain anchoring before the entry-level market closes further.

AI fluency without process fluency is an engine with no chassis. The power is real. But it has nowhere to go.

The responsibility does not rest with graduates alone. Industry must reinvent the apprenticeship. Universities must mandate embedded process exposure, not just AI coursework. Governments in Africa and globally must treat this workforce transition as the structural emergency it is — before the gap between AI-native tool fluency and domain-blind graduates becomes a permanent feature of the labour market.
