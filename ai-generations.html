AI Knows the Tool.
But Who Knows
the Process?

AI adoption favoured those already inside the machine. The Millennials and Gen X adapted out of survival. But what happens to the generation arriving after the machine already knows the job — and the one after that, which has never known anything else?

OI
Oluwafemi Ibukun Williams Petroleum Analyst · DevOps Learner · Writer
← Back to Home

Artificial intelligence has become the operating system of global business. It is not a feature. It is the infrastructure beneath everything — procurement, communication, analysis, design, logistics, compliance. And like any operating system, the people who understand it best are not those who read the manual. They are the ones who were already using the machine when the upgrade arrived.

That is the first thing we need to establish clearly: AI adoption happened from the inside out.

The Insider Advantage

When AI tools began to mature and proliferate — roughly from 2020 onward, accelerating dramatically through 2023 — the people who adopted them most effectively were not fresh graduates. They were working professionals. Millennials in their careers. Gen X managers mid-stride. People already embedded in real processes with real stakes.

Why? Because you cannot integrate AI into a process you do not already understand.

I work in the petroleum industry. I know what it takes to run a laboratory analysis, maintain quality documentation, ensure compliance with production standards. When I began using AI tools, I was not learning a new job — I was accelerating one I already knew. The AI compressed weeks of documentation work into hours. It did not replace my process knowledge. It amplified it.

"You cannot introduce AI into a process you are not already part of. Process knowledge is the prerequisite — and that is exactly what the new graduate does not yet have."

This is not a small distinction. It is the entire argument. The working professional adopted AI because they had something to attach it to. A framework. A workflow. Years of contextual understanding that told them where the tool fit and where it did not.

The Graduate Arrives Late — To a Changed Game

Now consider the fresh graduate in 2025. They emerge from a university system that, at best, offered theoretical AI literacy — courses about what AI is, perhaps some prompt engineering basics. What they were not taught, because universities cannot teach it, is domain process knowledge.

They arrive at the workplace to find that the entry-level tasks they were trained to perform have largely been absorbed by AI. The repetitive analysis work. The first-draft writing. The basic data processing. The tasks that used to build process fluency in junior staff — gone, or dramatically compressed.

This creates a paradox. The graduate needs to understand the process before they can use AI well. But the traditional pathway to process understanding — doing the work at entry level — has been shortened or eliminated by AI itself.

The very tasks that used to build competence in junior professionals are the same tasks AI has now automated. The ladder has not been removed — but several of the lower rungs have been cut away.

Three Generations. Three Different Problems.

Generation
Millennials & Gen X
Adapted

Already inside the process when AI arrived. Adopted out of survival necessity. Used AI to amplify existing knowledge. Disrupted, but not displaced — those who moved fast enough.

Generation
Gen Z Graduates
At Risk

Entering a workforce where entry-level tasks are automated. Theoretical AI training, minimal process experience. Must find new pathways to build the domain knowledge AI cannot replace.

Generation
Gen Alpha
Unknown Territory

AI-native from birth. Will likely operate with capabilities we cannot fully anticipate. The risk: fluent with the tool, but potentially alienated from the underlying processes the tool serves.

Gen Alpha: A Different Kind of Problem

Gen Alpha presents a challenge that is qualitatively different from anything before it. These children are not just comfortable with AI — they are shaped by it from their earliest years. Their relationship to information, problem-solving, and cognition will be fundamentally different from every generation that preceded them.

The concern is not that they will lack intelligence. It is that they may lack process grounding. An understanding of why things work the way they do — before the machine optimises them. The petroleum engineer who understands fluid dynamics before running a simulation is a different professional from one who can only interpret simulation outputs.

How do we guide a generation into process understanding when the processes themselves are increasingly invisible — handled silently by AI systems? This is not a rhetorical question. It is one that educators, employers, and policymakers need to answer with some urgency.

What the Degree Is Becoming

There is a harder truth underneath all of this: the traditional degree is being quietly devalued. Not abolished — but compressed. What once required four years of structured study to achieve can now be approximated in a fraction of the time by a skilled AI user with the right domain context.

This does not mean education is worthless. It means the form of education needs to change. The curriculum must shift from knowledge transfer — telling students what we know — to process immersion — putting students inside real workflows early, so they develop the contextual fluency that makes AI a tool rather than a crutch.

"The degree that does not produce process knowledge is producing something AI can already simulate. That is the uncomfortable reality African universities have not yet confronted."

For Africa especially, this matters enormously. We are sending graduates into a global labour market where the rules changed mid-game. The answer is not to fear AI, nor to chase it blindly. It is to rebuild education and professional onboarding around the one thing AI still cannot manufacture from scratch: earned process understanding.

A Closing Position

I am not against AI. I use it. I am building new skills around it. I see its extraordinary power in my own field. But power without context is dangerous — and context without experience is hollow.

The generation that will thrive in this new era is not the one that simply knows how to use AI. It is the one that knows what to use it for — and that knowledge only comes from being inside the work, understanding the stakes, and earning the right to hand it to a machine.

That is the gap. And if we do not close it intentionally, AI will not create the workforce of the future. It will create a workforce that operates the future without understanding it.
