A Contemplative Reflection

Beyond the God
I Imagined

On losing our images of God — and finding Him in the face of Christ

✦
“You have heard it said… but I say to you…” Matthew 5:21–22

One of the most challenging aspects of spiritual growth is discovering that many of the things we thought were God were actually our projections onto God.

We often inherit images of God from family, culture, church traditions, fears, and personal wounds. Sometimes God becomes a cosmic scorekeeper. Sometimes a distant ruler. Sometimes a disappointed parent who is never quite satisfied.

The Method of Jesus

Jesus spent much of His ministry dismantling these false images. Again and again He said, “You have heard it said… but I say to you.”

He was not abolishing truth. He was unveiling a deeper truth.

Show Us the Father

When Philip asked Jesus, “Show us the Father,” Jesus replied:

“Anyone who has seen me has seen the Father.” John 14:9

The ultimate revelation of God is not fear. It is Christ.

If an image of God contradicts the self-giving love revealed in Jesus, it deserves to be questioned. This can feel unsettling.

When Questions Feel Like Loss

Deconstruction often feels like losing God when, in reality, we may simply be losing our misconceptions about God. The good news is that Christ is not threatened by our questions.

Truth does not fear examination. Love does not fear honesty.

Sometimes faith grows not by adding new beliefs but by releasing old distortions.

Abba

The Aramaic world of Jesus emphasized relationship over abstraction. The word Jesus used for God, Abba, carries the intimacy of a beloved parent rather than the distance of a remote monarch.

Jesus did not come merely to teach doctrines about God. He came to reveal what God has always been like.

The Cross as Unveiling

The Cross becomes the ultimate unveiling:

God absorbs violence rather than inflicting it, forgives rather than retaliates, and restores rather than rejects.

A Pattern Through Scripture

Throughout Scripture, humanity repeatedly mistakes God’s character.

  • Adam hides.
  • Israel fears.
  • Jonah resents mercy.
  • The religious leaders reject grace.

Yet God continually reveals Himself as more compassionate than people imagined. The deeper pattern points toward Christ, who becomes the living interpretation of God’s heart.

What Dies, and What Lives

Many people fear that questioning inherited beliefs will destroy their faith. Sometimes it does destroy something. But often what dies is not faith itself.

What dies is a version of God built from fear, control, certainty, or exclusion. The resurrection pattern applies here too:

False images die. Deeper truth emerges.
Let Us Pray

Abba,
Give me courage to release every image of You
that is smaller than the love revealed in Christ.
Where fear has distorted my vision, bring light.
Where shame has hidden Your face, bring healing.
Where certainty has become an idol, teach me humility.
Lead me beyond the God I have imagined
into the God who truly is.

Amen
Contemplative Practice

Spend a few moments reflecting on this question:

What image of God am I carrying that looks different from Jesus?

Do not judge your answer. Simply hold it before Christ. Then sit quietly and repeat:

“Jesus, show me the Father’s heart.”

Allow that prayer to accompany you throughout the day.
