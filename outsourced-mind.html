The Outsourced Mind

Social media took our attention. AI is coming for our agency — and most of us are handing it over without a fight.

IBUKUN · PERSPECTIVES

There is a quieter attack underway, and it is not the one dominating headlines about job losses. Social media spent the last fifteen years re-engineering human attention — fragmenting it, monetising it, training us to flinch toward a notification rather than sit with a thought. That project succeeded. AI is now running a second, more consequential experiment: not on what we look at, but on whether we still think at all once something else is willing to think for us.

The evidence is no longer speculative. A 2025 study from Microsoft Research and Carnegie Mellon University surveyed 319 knowledge workers across occupations and examined 936 real-world instances of them using generative AI on the job. The finding was precise and uncomfortable: the more confidence a worker had in the AI's ability to complete a task, the less critical thinking they applied to the result. The relationship ran in the opposite direction for self-confidence — workers who trusted their own judgment engaged more critically with what the machine handed back, not less. Sixty-two percent of participants admitted to less critical thinking when using AI, especially on tasks they judged to be routine or low-stakes.

Read that carefully. It is not that AI made people less capable. It is that confidence in the tool became a substitute for the discipline of checking, and that substitution happened quietly enough that most people did not notice it happening to them.

You would not send someone else to the gym and expect your own body to change. Yet that is precisely the arrangement millions of knowledge workers have made with their minds.

The Shift, Not the Disappearance

The researchers were careful to note that critical thinking has not vanished from knowledge work — it has relocated. Where it once lived in information gathering, problem-solving from scratch, and direct task execution, it now needs to live in verification, integration, and stewardship: checking whether the AI's output is actually correct, adapting it to context the machine cannot see, and supervising a process rather than performing it. That relocation is the real danger, because it is a skill few people were ever trained to exercise deliberately, and fewer still are practising now that the tool offers a plausible-sounding shortcut past it.

A muscle that is never asked to work does not politely wait in reserve. It atrophies. The same is true of the specific cognitive muscle that catches an error, questions a premise, or notices that a confident answer is a wrong one.

Why Hobbies Are Not a Luxury

This is where a serious hobby stops being a leisure activity and becomes something closer to a nutritional requirement. Most of what fills modern attention — feeds, shorts, algorithmic recommendations — is built to be consumed without resistance. It asks nothing of you and gives back proportionally little. A hobby practised with any seriousness is the opposite kind of activity. It demands sustained focus, tolerates no shortcut, and punishes carelessness immediately and honestly: the chess position collapses, the dough fails to rise, the instrument produces a sound you did not intend, the essay draft does not hold together on a second read.

That friction is the point. A hobby is one of the few remaining spaces where a person is forced to complete the full loop the Microsoft study describes — attempt, evaluate, correct, attempt again — without an intermediary offering to do the evaluating on their behalf. It is deliberate practice for the exact faculty that AI overconfidence is quietly switching off elsewhere: the willingness to check your own work against reality rather than against a plausible-sounding shortcut.

Practised this way, a hobby is training. Chess trains the recognition of when a confident-looking line is actually a losing one. Writing by hand or without assistance trains the discomfort of sitting with an unfinished thought until it resolves. Building something physical trains the acceptance that a step skipped now shows up as a failure later, with no one else to blame. None of these transfer by accident — they transfer because they keep the verification muscle under load on a schedule the rest of modern life no longer provides.

The Discipline This Requires

None of this is an argument against using AI. It is an argument against using it the way most of the study's least critical participants did — treating fluency as proof of correctness, and convenience as a substitute for judgment. The workers who kept their critical faculties intact were not the ones who avoided the tool. They were the ones who trusted their own judgment enough to keep checking the machine's.

That trust in your own judgment is not innate. It is built the same way physical strength is built — through repeated, resistant, unglamorous practice, on something that will not do the thinking for you. If your only sustained mental effort each week happens inside a chat window, you are not exercising the muscle the moment will eventually demand. Keep something in your life that pushes back. Everything else is outsourcing that quietly becomes atrophy.

Study cited: Lee et al., "The Impact of Generative AI on Critical Thinking: Self-Reported Reductions in Cognitive Effort and Confidence Effects From a Survey of Knowledge Workers," Microsoft Research & Carnegie Mellon University, 2025.
